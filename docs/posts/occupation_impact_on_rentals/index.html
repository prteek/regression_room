Dataset

Additional information

Local authority for each LSOA in original dataset is added, to account for rental patterns that may be present at local area levels. Being in the same Local authority may affect neighbouring LSOAs introducing correlations in their data.

Since, the interest lies in proportion of rentership for each occupation, per occupation totals for each lsoa are added to the dataset.

Subsampling

Since, the dataset is very well compiled but is too large, we can work with reduced dataset and infer large scale effects from it. This reduction will allow speeding up the analysis and experimentation.
The subsampling process does the following:

  • Randomly sample 10 local authorities from 8 UK wide regions (e.g. West midlands, East of England etc.) excluding London region (since market behaviour may not generalise to other regions and create issues when data is mixed with others, due to very high counts)
  • Within each of the sampled local authorities sample 50 LSOAs at random
  • Get all the data in the sampled LSOAs

The above procedure is deliberate in that it results in balanced data among Local authorities to avoid issues with inference.
It goes without saying that this step means that Local Authorities and LSOAs need to be treated as random effects down the line (with appropriate heirarchy).
The procedure ensures sufficient counts of Local Authorities and LSOAs to be able to reliably capture these random effects.

Filtering

All the instances where Occupation is does not apply refer to the household reference person (HRP) not in full time employment a week before census.
Given the focus of the study on rental tenures, this can possibly correlate with the cases where HRP would be living rent free. This would not be of interest either, so this Occupation code is dropped from the dataset entirely.

Note:

  • Such an issue does not arise in (for example) 2011 census data because the data itself excludes HRP not in occupation
  • Occupation does not apply is the largest category in rental tenure data (~34%) (“Annex Table 1.5: Employment Status by Tenure,2021-22” 2023)
    • part-time work (~11% includes furloughed)
    • retired (~7%)
    • unemployed (~4%)
    • full-time education (~4%)
    • other inactive (~8%)
  • The private rented sector houses the highest proportion of non-UK nationals (74% of HRPs in the private rented sector are from the UK, compared to 92% of social renters and 96% of owner occupiers) (“English Housing Survey 2021 to 2022: Private Rented Sector” 2023)

EDA

There is still a long tail of population per lsoa. These LSOAs are located in dense Local authorities in cities like Manchester and Newcastle etc. This may lead to overdispersion issues.

`stat_bin()` using `bins = 30`. Pick better value `binwidth`.

At the level of LSOA the proportion of rentership can appear quite chaotic and noisy.

The variability in renting due to occupation is visible when looked at in aggregate. This also seems to relate to our intuition where highly skilled and professionals are driving less of the rental market since they may be better placed to afford to buy.

The observation holds and patterns become more clear when the data is aggregated at Local authority level. This means that a heirarchical consideration of geographies could be a sensible inclusion in the model.
